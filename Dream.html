An Analysis of I Have a Dream
I Have a Dream has become one of the most celebrated and remembered speeches in
American history and around the world. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., the author of this speech,
was a civil rights activist who worked hard to end racial injustice in the United States. On August
28 of 1963, Dr. King led a march on Washington D.C. where he gave his now famous speech
which has inspired millions of people around the world.
The moment in which Dr. King moved from a manuscript delivery to an extemporaneous
delivery was at around 6 minutes and 45 seconds when he said, But there is something that I
must say (King Jr.). This was the beginning of the switch in his delivery because of the
reaction of the crowd to the words in his previous paragraph. His rate of speaking became faster
with those words, which suggests that he was no longer reading a prepared speech. Before that
point, he had spoken slowly with a controlled pitch and longer pauses. However, his pitch then
increased, and his pauses became shorter between each statement. His passion also sounded
stronger after this point than it did before. All of this suggests that Dr. King had been following
his written speech prior to this point, but then decided to change his method of delivery.
I had not heard Dr. Kings speech completely before, so it was surprising to learn that he
had not planned to use those famous words. The parts that I had heard from his speech always

included those iconic statements, and the fact that they are still remembered globally made me
assume that he had carefully planned his delivery and impact with those words. However, I also
understand that sometimes the best speeches are the ones that are spoken from the heart with
passion without a pre-written speech to determine what to say. Despite my initial reaction, I am
no longer so surprised to know that he had not planned on using those words.
I do not think Dr. Kings speech would have had the same effect if he had followed his
pre-planned manuscript. At the start of his speech, I was personally feeling a little bored. His
initial delivery did not make me feel the passion that he had towards the racial justice cause. I
just did not feel any excitement or passion for what he was saying. However, after he switched
his delivery, I began to feel the energy and passion of his words which made me more engaged
with his speech. Without the extemporaneous part of his speech, I think Dr. King would have
failed to create the legacy and hope that those famous words now represent.
In conclusion, Dr. Kings spontaneous decision to give a more impromptu speech rather
than follow his preplanned manuscript helped create the memory that we all remember to this
day. His increased rate of speaking, his higher pitch, and his energy and passion as he spoke
towards the end of his speech helped create the appropriate mood and engage his audience on
that day, and for generations to come. Without his crucial decision to switch his mode of
delivery, we may not have had these iconic words to remember today.
